### 2.2 PCI-DSS v4.0.1

April 2026 marks a critical enforcement inflection point for the payment card industry:

- **All future-dated requirements from PCI-DSS v4.0.1 are now fully enforceable.** Organizations that treated the transition period as optional now face assessment findings, potential non-compliance designations, and increased transaction fees or processing restrictions.
- Key areas under scrutiny include **targeted risk analysis documentation, enhanced authentication mechanisms, client-side script management (Requirement 6.4.3), and automated log review mechanisms (Requirement 10.4.1.1).**
- Qualified Security Assessors (QSAs) are reporting increased finding rates in organizations that underinvested in implementation.

> **⚠️ Compliance Alert:** Any merchant or service provider that has not fully implemented all PCI-DSS v4.0.1 requirements — including those previously designated as future-dated best practices — is now operating in a **non-compliant state** and should initiate emergency remediation.

### 2.3 GDPR — European Data Protection

GDPR enforcement continues to mature and intensify in April 2026:

- **Record Enforcement Activity**: European Data Protection Authorities (DPAs) have sustained aggressive enforcement cadences, with cumulative fines exceeding €5.2 billion since inception. Q1–Q2 2026 actions have targeted **cross-border data transfers, cookie consent mechanisms, and AI training data legality**.
- **AI Act Intersection**: The EU AI Act's phased enforcement creates new compliance obligations that intersect with existing GDPR requirements, particularly around automated decision-making (Article 22), data minimization for AI training sets, and algorithmic transparency.
- **Schrems III Concerns**: Legal challenges to the EU-US Data Privacy Framework continue to generate uncertainty. Organizations relying solely on adequacy decisions for transatlantic transfers should maintain fallback Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs).

### 2.4 SOX — Financial Reporting Controls

Sarbanes-Oxley compliance complexity is increasing materially:

- **AI in Financial Reporting**: The adoption of AI and machine learning models in financial forecasting, revenue recognition, and internal controls is creating new categories of material weakness risk. Auditors are beginning to require **explainability documentation and model governance evidence** for AI-assisted financial processes.
- **IT General Controls (ITGC) Scrutiny**: Cloud migration and SaaS-heavy environments continue to challenge traditional ITGC models. SOC 1/SOC 2 report reliance requires enhanced Complementary User Entity Control (CUEC) documentation.
- **Material Weakness Disclosure Trends**: Public filings in Q1 2026 show a **12% year-over-year increase** in material weakness disclosures related to technology control failures.

### 2.5 CCPA/CPRA — California Privacy

California's privacy regime continues to set the national standard:

- **Automated Decision-Making Technology (ADMT) Regulations**: Finalized CPRA regulations around ADMT are creating new access, opt-out, and impact assessment requirements that affect any organization profiling California residents.
- **Enforcement Expansion**: The California Privacy Protection Agency (CPPA) has expanded enforcement staff and is actively investigating data broker practices, dark patterns in consent flows, and children's data processing.
- **National Ripple Effect**: Multiple states have enacted or updated comprehensive privacy legislation modeled on CCPA/CPRA, increasing the fragmented state compliance burden.

### 2.6 ISO 27001:2022 Transition

- **Transition Deadline Pressure**: Organizations holding ISO 27001:2013 certifications face expiration of legacy certificates. Certification bodies are reporting **scheduling backlogs** for transition audits.
- **Annex A Control Updates**: The restructured 93-control framework (from 114) with new controls covering threat intelligence, cloud security, data masking, and secure development lifecycle requires updated Statements of Applicability and control evidence.
